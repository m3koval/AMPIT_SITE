AI Chats Aren't Protected by Attorney-Client Privilege

A federal court just ruled on it. Here's what every Charlotte business needs to know.

By AMP IT Solutions · Charlotte, NC May 4, 2026 10 min read

In February 2026, Judge Jed Rakoff of the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York issued the first federal ruling of its kind: AI chat logs are not protected by attorney-client privilege and can be handed directly to prosecutors.

Federal Ruling · United States v. Heppner · No. 25-cr-00503-JSR

The case involved a defendant who used Claude by Anthropic to research his legal defense after receiving a federal grand jury subpoena. The FBI seized his devices. The government requested the AI-generated documents. His lawyers claimed privilege. The court said no.

Judge Rakoff called it a question of "first impression nationwide." Courts had not ruled on this before. Now they have — and the answer has implications far beyond one criminal case.

Why AI Chats Don't Qualify for Attorney-Client Privilege

Attorney-client privilege has three requirements. The court found AI chats fail all three.

1.

There was no attorney involved.

Claude is not a lawyer. It holds no law license, owes no duty of loyalty, and cannot form an attorney-client relationship. The court noted that all recognized privileges require "a trusting human relationship" — and that relationship cannot exist between a user and an AI platform.

2.

There was no confidentiality.

Anthropic's privacy policy — the one users consent to when they sign up — states that the platform collects user inputs and outputs, uses that data to train its models, and reserves the right to disclose data to third parties, including government regulatory authorities. The court found there was no reasonable expectation of confidentiality.

3.

It was not for the purpose of obtaining legal advice.

The defendant used Claude on his own initiative, not at his attorney's direction. What matters for privilege is who directed the communication — not who the user intended to share results with later.

The bottom line: sharing privileged information with a consumer AI platform is treated the same as sharing it with any other third party. That privilege is waived.

This Is Not Just a Legal Problem. It Is a Business Problem.

You do not have to be under federal investigation for this to matter. Right now, someone on your team is probably:

  • Pasting a client proposal into ChatGPT to clean it up
  • Asking Claude to summarize a vendor contract or NDA
  • Using Google Gemini to draft a termination letter or job offer
  • Running financials through Microsoft Copilot without realizing it is connected to a personal account
  • Dropping meeting notes into Notion AI or Grammarly without thinking twice

None of that feels dangerous. And most of it is not — if it is happening on the right platform, under the right terms. If it is happening on a personal or consumer-tier account, that information is sitting on a server retained for years, potentially reviewed by humans, and — as a federal court just confirmed — potentially handed to the other side.

For a law firm, that is a state bar ethics violation waiting to happen. For a CPA or financial advisor, that is a FINRA or HIPAA exposure. For any business, it is a competitive intelligence risk you never agreed to.

The Pro Plan Does Not Fix This

Here is the assumption most business owners make: "We pay for the good version, so we are protected." That assumption is wrong — and the Heppner ruling confirms it.

Claude Pro $20/month
Consumer tier terms of service. Opting out of AI training does not remove Anthropic's right to disclose your data to authorities under legal process — which is exactly what the Heppner court relied on.
ChatGPT Plus / Pro $20–$200/month
OpenAI's privacy policy contains the same provisions. Data can be disclosed in response to legal process regardless of your subscription level.
Google Gemini Advanced Consumer plan
Same story — and Gemini goes further. It now integrates with Gmail, Google Drive, and Google Calendar by default for US users. Your emails, files, and meetings are accessible to the AI without most people realizing it.
Microsoft Copilot Free / Personal
The free and personal versions operate under Microsoft's consumer privacy terms — not the commercial data protection agreements that come with a business license. They are not the same product.

A consumer AI subscription buys you features. It does not buy you a contract. It does not buy you confidentiality. And it will not protect you when a subpoena arrives.

Where the Real Protection Lives

The protection does exist. It lives in business and enterprise agreements — not consumer plans. The difference the Heppner court examined was exactly this: is there a signed contract with enforceable data commitments?

Microsoft 365 Copilot Business / Enterprise
Your data stays inside your Microsoft tenant. Not used to train models. Covered under Microsoft's commercial data protection commitments — the same framework your email and files already operate under. If your team uses Microsoft 365 today, this may already be closer than you think.
Google Workspace + Gemini Business / Enterprise
Enterprise-grade data protections. Not used for training. Not reviewed by humans. Governed by Google's commercial terms — not the consumer privacy policy your personal Gmail lives under.
Claude for Work / Enterprise Business tier
Explicitly excluded from Anthropic's consumer privacy policy and the legal disclosure rights the Heppner court relied on.
ChatGPT Enterprise / Team Business tier
Data is not used for model training and is covered under OpenAI's commercial data agreements — not the consumer terms that apply to Plus and Pro.

What This Means If You Are a Law Firm, CPA, or Financial Advisor

You already operate under obligations most businesses do not have. State bar ethics rules, FINRA recordkeeping requirements, HIPAA — all of them assume your client data stays where you put it.

Shadow AI — employees using personal accounts for ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or other tools outside of company-managed systems — does not care about your obligations. It just does what it is told.

You do not need to ban AI. Your competitors are using it and moving faster. What you need is to make sure your team is using it on the right side of that line.

Four Things to Do Right Now

  1. Find out what your team is actually using. Most business owners are surprised by the answer. Shadow AI — personal accounts for ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Grammarly, Notion AI, and others — is far more common than most companies realize.
  2. Establish an approved AI stack. Decide which tools are permitted, on which accounts, under which licensing tier. A personal ChatGPT account and a ChatGPT Enterprise account are not the same product. Same name. Very different legal standing.
  3. Put a written policy in place. Even a simple one. "Approved AI tools only. Here is the list. Here is why." That document protects your business and gives your team clear direction.
  4. Get your licensing right. If your team already uses Microsoft 365, Copilot may be available under business-grade protections you are not yet using. If you use Google Workspace, Gemini's enterprise protections are built in — but only if the account is configured and managed correctly. The tool may not be the problem. The setup might be.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does this ruling affect businesses, or just criminal defendants?

The Heppner ruling came from a criminal case, but the reasoning applies broadly — including civil litigation, regulatory investigations, and internal compliance reviews. If sensitive business information goes into a consumer AI, it may be discoverable in any legal proceeding.

What if employees opt out of AI training?

Opting out of model training is a separate setting from legal disclosure rights. Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google all reserve the right to disclose user data in response to legal process regardless of training preferences. Opting out of training does not create confidentiality.

Does an enterprise plan fully protect us?

Enterprise agreements significantly reduce the risk. They establish contractual data protections and exclude your data from training. They do not make you immune to subpoenas — no contract does — but they establish a reasonable expectation of confidentiality that consumer plans explicitly do not.

What is shadow AI?

Shadow AI refers to employees using AI tools — often personal accounts for ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Grammarly, or others — outside of company-approved, company-managed systems. It is one of the fastest-growing IT security and compliance risks for small businesses right now.
