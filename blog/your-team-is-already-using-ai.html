Your Team Is Already Using AI. Here's What Nobody Told Them.

By AMP IT · Charlotte, NC April 29, 2026 8 min read

A federal court just handed down a ruling that every business owner in Charlotte needs to hear — not because it's legal drama, but because it quietly describes what's probably happening inside your company right now.

Federal Ruling · 2026

In United States v. Heppner (2026), a judge ruled that conversations with AI assistants like Claude and ChatGPT carry no legal confidentiality protection. The platform can be compelled to hand those conversations over. The reasoning? The terms of service you — or your employees — clicked through and almost certainly never read say they can.

That ruling isn't an outlier. It's a warning shot.

The "Pro Plan" Trap

Here's what most small business owners assume: "We pay for the good version, so we're protected."

That assumption is wrong — and it's one we're seeing play out across law firms, CPA practices, and financial advisory offices right here in Charlotte.

ChatGPT Plus $20/month
Conversations used for training by default. You have to manually opt out — and that doesn't fix the legal exposure anyway.
Claude Pro $20/month
Same story. After a policy change in late 2025, consumer plans — Free, Pro, and Max — are all treated the same when it comes to data training and legal disclosure.
Gemini Advanced $20/month
Identical situation. And Gemini goes one step further: it now connects to your Gmail by default for US users. Your email history. In the AI. Without a conversation.

Paying more for a consumer AI plan buys you features. It does not buy you privacy. It does not buy you a contract. And it does not protect you when a subpoena shows up.

What Your Employees Are Actually Doing Right Now

Let's make this concrete. Right now, someone on your team is probably:

  • Pasting a client proposal into ChatGPT to "clean it up"
  • Asking Claude to summarize a vendor contract or NDA
  • Using Gemini to draft a termination letter or job offer
  • Running client financials through AI to prep for a meeting

None of that feels dangerous. And most of it isn't — if it's happening on the right platform, with the right account, under the right terms.

If it's happening through a personal Gmail or a consumer subscription? That information is sitting on a server. It can be retained for up to five years. It can be reviewed by humans. And as a federal court just confirmed, it can be handed over.

For a law firm, that's a state bar issue. For a CPA or financial advisor, that's a FINRA or HIPAA issue. For any business, it's a competitive intelligence risk you didn't sign up for.

There Is a Line — It's Just Not Where Most People Think It Is

The protection does exist. It lives in business and enterprise plans — not consumer subscriptions.

Microsoft 365 + Copilot Business / Enterprise
Your data stays in your Microsoft tenant. Not used to train models. Covered under Microsoft's commercial data protection commitments — the same framework your email and files already live in. If you're already paying for M365, this is closer than you think.
Google Workspace + Gemini Business / Enterprise
Enterprise-grade data protections. Submissions not used for training, not reviewed by humans, protected under Google's commercial terms — not the consumer privacy policy your personal Gmail account lives under.
Claude for Work / Enterprise Business tier
Explicitly excluded from Anthropic's consumer training policy and the legal disclosure exposure that comes with it.

The difference between consumer and business tier isn't just features — it's a contract with actual data commitments. Not a checkbox. Not a toggle buried in settings. A signed agreement.

What This Means If You're a Law Firm, CPA, or Financial Advisor

You already operate under obligations that most businesses don't. State bar ethics rules, FINRA recordkeeping, HIPAA — all of them assume your client data stays where you put it.

Shadow AI — employees using personal or consumer AI tools without your knowledge — doesn't care about your obligations. It just does what it's told.

You don't need to ban AI. Your competitors are using it and getting faster, and if you're not, you're falling behind. What you need is to make sure the tools being used inside your business are operating under terms that actually protect you and your clients.

What to Do About It

Three things, in order:

  1. Find out what your team is actually using. Most business owners are surprised. It's rarely nothing.
  2. Decide which tools are approved — and make sure they're on business-tier accounts with proper data agreements in place.
  3. Get a policy in writing. Even a simple one. "Use approved tools only. Here's the list." That conversation protects you.

Most small businesses in Charlotte haven't done any of this — not because they don't care, but because it moved fast and nobody handed them a playbook.
